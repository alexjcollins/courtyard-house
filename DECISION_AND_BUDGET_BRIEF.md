# Courtyard House Decision And Budget Brief

This file is the working reference for what needs to be decided for the house, what needs to be decided early enough to inform the architect, and what budget should be carried against each part of the build.

It is written for novice self-build clients who need a practical control document rather than a contractor bill of quantities.

## Purpose

- Turn the house into a complete decision schedule instead of a loose list of categories.
- Show what decisions matter now, before technical design and tender work moves too far.
- Assign realistic baseline budgets to each package so client selections can be made without drifting away from the overall build budget.

## Budget Context

- Target construction budget used in this brief: `£700,000 ex VAT`
- Contingency: excluded from this brief
- Professional fees: excluded from this brief
- Courtyard long-term fit-out and landscaping beyond essential building interfaces: excluded from this brief

## External Reality Check

This control budget is tight but plausible if the project stays disciplined.

For context:

- Homebuilding’s 2026 guide says a typical UK self-build often sits around `£1,750-£3,500/m²` ex VAT depending on design, method, and specification. For a `250 sqm` house, `£700,000` is about `£2,800/m²` including the garage, which is viable but not generous for a high-glazing South East scheme. Source: [Homebuilding self-build guide](https://www.homebuilding.co.uk/advice/self-build)
- Their 2026 cost guide also notes that raft foundations for a `200-250m²` self-build can vary significantly depending on site conditions and that foundations plus ground conditions are a major cost driver. Source: [How much will it cost to build a house in 2026?](https://www.homebuilding.co.uk/advice/how-much-does-it-cost-to-build-a-house)
- Energy Saving Trust’s current guidance says wet underfloor heating is typically around `£85-£110/m²`, and air source heat pumps are generally the standard low-carbon heating route in UK homes. Sources: [Underfloor heating](https://energysavingtrust.org.uk/advice/underfloor-heating), [Air source heat pumps](https://energysavingtrust.org.uk/advice/air-source-heat-pumps/)

Inference:

- Your budget can support a strong house, but not an everything-upgraded house.
- The big sliders, courtyard form, cladding, and room quality mean you need to be deliberate about where the “good money” goes.
- Joinery, bathrooms, glazing detailing, and decorative finishes are the easiest places to overspend.

## What Must Be Fixed With The Architect Now

These are the decisions that should be resolved early because they change structure, service routes, thermal detailing, compliance, or tender pricing.

### 1. Site, substructure, and shell

- Demolition scope for the existing Nissen hut and old foundations
- Final foundation strategy:
  - raft slab build-up
  - insulation position
  - service penetrations
  - edge thickening / load points
- Finished floor levels:
  - house
  - garage
  - courtyard thresholds
- Courtyard drainage strategy
- Surface water attenuation and inspection chamber layout
- Underground incoming services route and entry position
- Meter and service cupboard location
- Structural grid and steel design assumptions
- Timber infill / wall build-up strategy around the steel frame
- Roof build-ups:
  - pitched roofs
  - flat warm roof links
- Rainwater goods strategy:
  - visible
  - concealed
- Airtightness line and where it is maintained at complex junctions
- Garage thermal line:
  - fully within conditioned envelope
  - thermally separated but insulated

### 2. External envelope and openings

- Exact glazing schedule:
  - two 5m sliders
  - large guest bedroom window
  - three other 1.5m windows
  - two 1.5m master bedroom windows
  - 1m utility window
  - 1m powder room window
  - corner window
  - front door glazing assembly
- Opening types for each window:
  - fixed
  - side-hung
  - tilt/turn
  - sliding
- Door handing and threshold details
- Front door composition and level of entrance expression
- Garage door type
- Cladding species and fixing method
- Soffit and reveal finish
- Plinth / base detail
- Solar control / overheating approach for the large glazed openings

Relevant compliance items to remember:

- Ventilation design must satisfy Part F. Source: [Approved Document F](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ventilation-approved-document-f)
- Large glazed new dwellings must account for overheating risk under Part O. Source: [Approved Document O](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/overheating-approved-document-o)

### 3. Services strategy

- ASHP location:
  - acoustic impact
  - appearance
  - pipe run length
- Plant room arrangement:
  - hot water cylinder
  - manifolds
  - MVHR unit
  - distribution boards
  - treatment / filter kit if required
- Heating strategy by zone
- Whether underfloor heating is full-house or partial
- Ventilation strategy:
  - MVHR yes/no
  - supply and extract locations
  - duct routes
  - ceiling void coordination
- Consumer unit, data hub, router, and comms cupboard location
- Broadband ducting and entry
- Smart meter position
- EV charging route and capacity

Useful early-stage regulation items:

- New homes should be designed to be smart-meter ready. Source: [GOV.UK smart meter guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/design-and-construct-new-builds-to-enable-smart-meter-installations/guidance-on-designing-and-constructing-new-builds-to-be-smart-meter-ready-accessible-webpage)
- New homes in England need gigabit-ready infrastructure and connectivity planning. Source: [New build connectivity guidance](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/new-build-connectivity-information-for-developers)

### 4. Internal planning that affects build cost now

- Bathroom locations and drainage stack strategy
- Whether bathrooms are kept stacked and compact
- Stair form and whether it becomes a feature stair
- Joinery zones that need to be built into the architecture:
  - wardrobes
  - utility storage
  - media joinery
  - entrance storage
- Kitchen layout:
  - island position
  - tall unit wall
  - sink location
  - hob extraction strategy
- Utility room layout and appliance stacking
- Door widths and whether any doors become taller / wider than standard

## Where You Should Be Strict On Budget

- glazing upgrades beyond the quoted package
- feature stairs
- full-height stone and premium tile bathrooms
- fully bespoke wardrobes everywhere
- overly complex lighting schemes
- decorative wall finishes
- unnecessary smart-home systems

## Where Spending More Usually Pays Back

- getting the shell, airtightness, and thermal detailing right
- getting glazing layout and threshold details right
- enough sockets and sensible data provision
- good plant-room coordination
- well-designed kitchen layout
- disciplined bathroom layouts
